Score. Confidence. Target. One engine.

The scores you just saw are not opinions. Each maturity level has a defined set of evidence markers; the confidence band is literally the marker-coverage rate. Reference basis · why these are the five drivers
The five-driver framework is the consensus used by software-M&A diligence teams when valuing services-firms-with-product transitions. Each driver is established and tracked publicly in industry research (SaaS Capital Index, Software Equity Group quarterly M&A reports, PitchBook, Mergermarket), Big 4 advisory diligence frameworks (KPMG / Deloitte / EY / PwC), and practitioner valuation literature (Damodaran, Bain Global PE Report, McKinsey Software/SaaS). Pivot · What this means for Gysho

Your stake in this.
Upside-first.

Before we walk the mechanics. Before we acknowledge the cost. The honest view of what Gysho receives at close, what flows after close, and what stays untouched. The structure is designed so Gysho walks away ahead, not just protected.

At close
What you receive
  • Platform licence fee. Material, paid at close, entirely Gysho's.
  • Share of Foundation licence fee. Co-licensor revenue split with Safwai, sized to contribution.
  • Validated reference customer. Regulated PE-advisory deployment of your substrate — the credential you do not have today.
  • Confidentiality and IP indemnity covenants. Your exposure is contractually contained.
Ongoing
What flows after close
  • Optional update-licence revenue. If PoE elects to take Foundation/Platform updates from your parent fork, recurring fees flow to you.
  • MSA infrastructure share. Where Safwai operates the engine for PoE on your substrate, infrastructure economics share back to Gysho.
  • Multi-product enablement. Each Safwai vertical deal (compliance, M&A diligence, regulatory readiness, ERP cutover) re-uses your Platform. PoE is the first commercial deployment, not the last.
Keeps
What stays yours
  • Full ownership of the Platform. Nothing about the substrate transfers to PoE. They licence; you keep the asset.
  • Parent-fork evolution. Your fork keeps moving. PoE's snapshot stagnates without you. They depend on you for relevance over time.
  • Every non-PE-advisory field. Compliance, regulatory readiness, M&A diligence, ERP cutover, sector regulators — every other vertical stays unencumbered.
  • Authorship credit on the Foundation. The patterns you co-authored remain yours in attribution and in moral right.

Three columns. Three honest views. Gysho gains a paid reference and ongoing economics; gives temporary disclosure into one field; keeps the substrate and every other market intact. The asymmetry favours the deal.

04 · Our proposed approach

Source code at close.
Three mechanisms. One outcome.

PoE gets full ownership of the layers that need to live on PoE's balance sheet, and full possession of the source code for the layers underneath, under licence terms drafted so the asset is functionally equivalent to ownership for diligence purposes. The Instance is not technically separable from Foundation and Platform — so PoE receives all of it at close.

Layer 1
Rubric
Stays with PoE
Already PoE's. Documented and confirmed in the deal. Safwai/Gysho granted a limited operational licence to use the rubric only as required to run the engine under the MSA.
Layer 2
Instance
Full assignment

Layer 3
Foundation
Snapshot + Licence
Complete source code snapshot delivered to PoE at close, under a perpetual paid-up irrevocable transferable licence. Scope-limited to PE advisory. PoE can run independently from Day 1. Future updates available under optional separate licence.
Layer 4
Platform
Snapshot + Licence
Same mechanism as Foundation. Complete source code snapshot at close. Same four licence conditions. Same field limitation. PoE has the substrate code in their possession from Day 1.
The four conditions on the Foundation and Platform licences
Perpetual
No term limit. No expiry. No renewal required.
Paid-up
Single consideration at close. No ongoing fees or royalties.
Irrevocable
Cannot be terminated for any reason. Breach remedies are damages only.
Transferable
Assignable to PoE's acquirer without consent or consent fee.
04 · The contract architecture

Three parties. Two mechanisms.
One closing.

Assignment and licence are different legal mechanisms producing different outcomes. The deal uses one of each, between three parties. Every relationship has a defined direction, mechanism, and consideration — nothing about who-pays-whom-for-what is left implicit.

Mechanism 01 · Assignment
Full transfer of ownership. Title moves. PoE can use, modify, sublicence, or resell the asset, subject only to the field limitation flowing from the underlying Foundation and Platform licences. Used for: Instance.
Mechanism 02 · Licence (perpetual · paid-up · irrevocable · transferable)
Grant of use rights without title transfer. The four conditions make it functionally equivalent to ownership for diligence purposes — capitalised on PoE's balance sheet as an intangible asset. Used for: Foundation and Platform.
05 · Why it satisfies

Every requirement.
Every diligence test.

Mapping the proposed approach back against the six PoE requirements (section 01) and the five buyer-diligence drivers (section 03). The structure is designed to land each item, not most of them.

PoE's six requirements
✓
Balance sheet ownership. Instance fully assigned. Foundation and Platform paid-up licences capitalise on the balance sheet as intangible assets under ASC 350 / IAS 38.
✓
Exit-ready in 3–5 years. Full source-code possession at close. Diligence package complete from Day 1. Acquirer inherits the licence by automatic transfer.
✓
Product revenue multiple. Paid-up licences preserve software margin. Engine subscription revenue is contracted, recurring, separable from advisory fees.
✓
No exit-disrupting resale limits. Licence transferable to acquirer without consent. Field limitation matches the buyer's thesis (PE advisor buying PE-advisory specialist).
✓
Scalable beyond consultant hours. Same engine across N portcos, M funds. Marginal customer cost = marginal compute. No licence-per-customer escalation.
✓
Not a white label. PoE owns the Instance outright and has source-code possession of Foundation and Platform. Not building Safwai/Gysho value — building PoE's value.
The five diligence drivers
✓
Recurring product revenue. PoE owns the Instance, controls commercial terms, books revenue. Subscription model unconstrained by upstream licence economics.
✓
Customer stickiness. PoE owns all customer evidence, history, configurations. Switching cost accrues to PoE. NRR > 100% is mechanical.
✓
Software margin profile. Paid-up licences mean no royalty bleed. Marginal cost is compute and storage only. Gross margin approaches 75–85% on engine revenue.
✓
Defensible IP asset. Source code in PoE's repositories. "Operate without seller" test passes today. Four-condition licence treated as equivalent to ownership in M&A diligence.
✓
Scalability + differentiation. Software architecture supports N-customer scaling. Field exclusivity inside PE advisory: PoE is unique in their market.

Eleven items in section 01 + 03. Eleven check marks here. The structure is not "good enough." It is engineered against the specific tests PoE and PoE's acquirer will run.

06 · Safeguards for Gysho

Disclosure is real.
Six provisions make it manageable.

Some know-how leakage is the price of admission for any structure that delivers genuine ownership characteristics to PoE. The mitigation is not pretending it isn't there — it is making it as small as possible. The licence carries six provisions that compress the exposure to a level Gysho can accept as the cost of unlocking the deal.

01 · Authorised access
Headcount cap on PoE personnel
The licence grants source-code access to a defined number of named PoE personnel only. The cap is expandable by mutual agreement, not unilateral request.
02 · Audit trail
All access logged and audited
Every source-code access is logged. Gysho has the right to conduct periodic audits to confirm that only authorised personnel have access and that no third party does.
03 · Confidentiality
Liquidated damages on breach
Strong confidentiality provisions with damages liquidated for material breach. Not just an obligation — a pre-quantified consequence that the breaching party knows in advance.
04 · Disclosure perimeter
Limited downstream sharing
PoE may disclose source code only to (a) its lawyers, accountants, and bankers under existing professional duties, and (b) a buyer's diligence team under a customary diligence NDA. No other parties.
05 · Personnel non-compete
Engineers cannot rotate to competitors
PoE personnel who have seen Foundation or Platform code are subject to a defined-period non-compete preventing them from joining a Gysho competitor or contributing to a competing platform.
06 · Use restriction
Cannot build a competing platform
The licence prohibits PoE from using the source-code knowledge to build a competing platform, or to develop the Foundation patterns for use outside the PE-advisory field.

These provisions do not eliminate trade-secret exposure. They reduce it to a manageable risk — small enough that Gysho can accept it as the cost of unlocking a deal that genuinely delivers PoE's exit-value need.

07 · Alternatives considered

Four other paths.
Each one breaks something.

These structures were considered and rejected. Transparency on why each fails the test matters — if the structures look broadly similar, the reasons for choosing among them are the only thing that matters.

Rejected
Option A
Licence only, with source-code escrow on trigger
PoE receives Instance source code. Foundation and Platform stay exclusively with Gysho, released to PoE only on insolvency, breach, or abandonment. Operational continuity is hypothetical.
Why rejected: The "operate without the seller" diligence test fails today, only passes hypothetically. Acquirer applies meaningful discount (~20–40% on the licensed-asset portion). The Instance is technically inseparable from Foundation and Platform — treating those as held-by-seller makes the whole asset diligence-thin. PoE's exit multiple is materially impaired.
Rejected
Option C
Joint venture / NewCo
A new entity jointly owned by PoE and Safwai/Gysho. Holds the entire operational stack in the PE-advisory field. On PoE's exit, the buyer acquires the entity.
Why rejected: Cleanest exit story, but over-engineered for a first transaction. Adds governance complexity (technology direction, hiring, vendor selection), tax complexity across jurisdictions, and months of legal set-up before deployment. Right answer if both sides want long-term equity alignment; wrong answer for a one-time IP-asset transaction.
Rejected
Option D
Full assignment of Foundation and Platform
PoE buys outright ownership of the entire stack — Instance, Foundation, Platform. Gysho's substrate is transferred. Safwai's pattern library is transferred.
Why rejected: Destroys Gysho's multi-product platform. Forecloses every other vertical product Safwai is building. Hands a PE-advisory firm an LLM-orchestration platform they have no team to maintain. PoE's eventual acquirer sees a PE-advisor with a half-maintained AI platform sitting inside it — depresses the multiple, raises it. No rational party wants this outcome.
Rejected
Option E
White label arrangement
Safwai builds and operates a PoE-branded engine. PoE pays per engagement or per portco. The IP stays with Safwai. PoE presents it as their own product.
Why rejected: Explicitly identified as the failed model by Daniel from prior experience — PoE ends up building Safwai's value, not PoE's. At exit, PoE has revenue but no defensible IP asset. The acquirer discovers an outsourced engine and prices PoE as a services firm. Fails every PoE requirement.
