+ Workflow Automation : Dutch Logistics Provider — Bill of Lading Reconciliation Logistics · Warehouse Operations · Cross-border Trade ✓ Production

The problem. A Dutch logistics service provider operates a high-volume warehouse receiving inbound container shipments from international suppliers — including significant volumes from China. Each inbound pallet arrives with a bill of lading that must be reconciled against the corresponding goods receipt note in the warehouse management system before the receipt can be confirmed and goods cleared into stock. The process was almost entirely manual: a warehouse administrator had to translate the document (often from Chinese), interpret the contents, locate the matching GRN in the back-office WMS, manually link the two records, and process the confirmation of receipt. Fifteen minutes of administrative labour per container, multiplied across thousands of containers per year — on a process that had to complete before the goods could be put away.

The underlying complication. This was not a documentation problem in isolation. It was a closed-loop reconciliation problem spanning a multilingual external document, a translation step, an interpretation step, a system lookup, a record-linking action, and a system write-back. No off-the-shelf OCR product solves this end-to-end. Hiring more multilingual administrators doesn't scale. Mistranslations, mistyped reference numbers, and mismatched line items propagated downstream into stock accuracy, invoicing, and supplier reconciliation.

The solution. An agentic document automation system that executes the entire reconciliation loop without human intervention. For each inbound pallet:

  • Bill of lading loaded at point of receipt — no administrator opens the document.
  • AI reads and translates the document — including Chinese-language source content.
  • Structured data extracted — supplier, item references, quantities, consignment metadata.
  • WMS queried — corresponding goods receipt note located and linked to the inbound record.
  • Confirmation written back into the WMS — loop closed without human keystrokes.

No administrator opens the document. No translator is in the loop. No one types reference numbers between systems. Every action is logged with a full audit trail.

Outcomes: 15 minutes of administrative time saved per container · administrative errors reduced through consistent automated extraction and reconciliation · in production since 2024 as an ongoing managed monthly service — the client trusts the system with live warehouse receiving operations. Engagement model: £2,900 one-off start-up · £4,450/month full-stack managed service (platform, maintenance, continuous improvement). This is a reference case for the recurring-revenue annuity model: an AI system deployed, trusted, and paid for every month — not a one-off project.

The pattern is reusable. Any "foreign document arrives → interpret → reconcile against system of record → write back confirmation" workflow is solvable with the same composable stack: file parsing, LLM + translation, embeddings, and system integrations. Immediately applicable in GCC port and customs operations (multilingual freight documentation at high transaction volumes), FMCG inbound logistics, regulated industries requiring a full audit trail per document-to-system step, and any high-volume back-office process where an unstructured external document must be matched and confirmed against an enterprise system without human keystrokes.

Built and operated on the SAFWAI / Gysho modular composable agentic AI stack. Client is unnamed for external reference — referred to as "a Dutch logistics provider" or "a European supply chain provider" in customer-facing materials. Production engagement, not a proof of concept.

+ Legal OS — AI-Powered Practice Operations Boutique Legal Firms · GCC primary PoC Scoped

The problem. A boutique legal firm's most valuable asset is the expertise of its partners. But a disproportionate share of partner and associate time goes to work that is not expertise: chasing documents, checking deadlines, drafting first cuts of things that follow templates, updating clients who just want to know what is happening, searching for a clause the firm has negotiated before. Hiring more lawyers does not fix this — it scales the cost of the problem. It is a process problem with a precise solution.

The design principle. The lawyer's judgment is the product; everything else is infrastructure. AI never acts consequentially without attorney approval — no communication is sent, no document is issued, no invoice is raised without a lawyer reviewing first. And the lawyer never needs to leave the system to do their work.

Two-layer architecture. The system is built from two component types, each chosen for what it does best:

  • Bespoke AI layer (SAFWAI platform) — specialised agents for research, drafting from the firm's own precedents, time capture, deadline monitoring, client comms, and knowledge surfacing. Agents share a common matter context; an orchestrator enforces review gates and maintains a full audit trail. Built specifically for the firm, on the firm's own documents, in the firm's sovereign environment.
  • Commodity layer — Xero/QuickBooks for accounting, SharePoint/Google Drive for documents, a lightweight court-calendar tool for the docket. Proven, inexpensive, API-connected. Lawyers never enter these systems day-to-day; the SAFWAI portal reads from them in real time and surfaces everything in one unified dashboard. Approved time entries and invoices post back automatically.
